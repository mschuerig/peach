📋 **John (Product Manager):** WHY are we shipping this? Because it fills a real gap — and the foundation is *solid*. Let me cut through the fluff. We've got all 7 epics closed, 19 stories delivered, zero third-party dependencies, and an adaptive algorithm that actually converges. That's not nothing. But let's be honest about what "solid starting point" means versus "ready for musicians."

The core training loop works. The adaptive difficulty works. Profile visualization works. Settings work. Localization in two languages — done. But here's what I keep coming back to: **we haven't validated with a single real user yet.** The seamless playback issue is a red flag — if users converge to sub-2-cent in Peach but plateau at 5 cents in InTune, our training might be giving musicians false confidence. That's the opposite of our "training, not testing" mission. We need to understand if we're actually *training* pitch discrimination or just training our specific task format.

The signed mean investigation (`hotfix-investigate-signed-mean`) is also more than a technical curiosity — it's a **product question**. If our perceptual profile can't distinguish "this person always hears sharp" from "this person has good accuracy," the profile visualization is misleading. And we're building trust on that visualization.

---

🏗️ **Winston (Architect):** The architecture is, frankly, in good shape for what it is. Protocol-based service layer, proper dependency injection via SwiftUI environment, clean separation of concerns. The `TrainingSession` state machine is well-bounded. Zero external dependencies is a deliberate and correct choice for an app this focused.

That said, there are things that should be addressed before this goes in front of real users:

1. **The `PerceptualProfileKey` living in `ProfileScreen.swift`** — this is a small thing but it's the kind of misplacement that metastasizes. It should live alongside `PerceptualProfile.swift`. The pattern is already established with `TrendAnalyzerKey`. This is a 5-minute fix that prevents confusion later.

2. **The sine wave audio source.** For a v1, it's fine — it's precise, deterministic, and zero-dependency. But musicians train their ears on *timbres*, not sine waves. The architecture is ready for swappable sound sources (the `NotePlayer` protocol is there), but this is likely the single biggest gap between "tech demo" and "useful tool for musicians."

3. **No persistence of the convergence chain state.** The Kazez chain resets on app restart. The `PerceptualProfile` rebuilds from stored `ComparisonRecord`s, but the current difficulty level (last cent offset) isn't persisted. That means every time a user opens the app, they're back at 100 cents until the profile bootstraps. For serious users, this is jarring.

As John noted, the seamless playback question is architecturally simple to address — a configurable delay between note1 and note2 in `TrainingSession` — but the *right* default requires user research.

---

🎨 **Sally (UX Designer):** Oh, I've been waiting to talk about this. Let me paint you a picture of our user's *journey* right now...

A musician — let's say a cellist — downloads Peach. They open the app, they see the Start Screen with a profile preview that's empty. They tap "Start Training." Two tones play back to back. They tap Higher or Lower. They get feedback. And then... it just *keeps going*. Forever. No sessions, no goals, no sense of progress within a sitting. That's the design philosophy — "training, not testing" — and I love the purity of it. But...

**Here's what worries me:**

- **Onboarding is nonexistent.** There's an Info screen, but nothing guides the user through their first comparison. Musicians aren't necessarily tech-savvy. What does the confidence band mean? What are "cents"? Most musicians know "sharp" and "flat," not "±12 cents."
- **The profile visualization is powerful but potentially opaque.** The piano keyboard + confidence band is gorgeous for *us*, but will a horn player intuitively understand a log-scale chart of their pitch discrimination threshold across MIDI notes? We need to translate this into language musicians actually use.
- **No sense of session or achievement.** I know we deliberately avoided gamification, but there's a difference between gamification and *acknowledgment*. Even a simple "You've trained for 5 minutes today" or "Your average threshold improved from 8 cents to 6 cents this week" would give users a reason to come back.
- **The haptic-only wrong-answer feedback** — on iPad there's no haptic engine. What happens there? Is there any visual reinforcement beyond the feedback indicator?

I want to be clear: the interaction design of the core loop — hear two notes, tap a button — is beautifully simple. That's a strength. But the *surrounding experience* needs to meet musicians where they are.
